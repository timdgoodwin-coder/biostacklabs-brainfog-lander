Why it happens

5 everyday things that drain your mental sharpness, and what's actually going on

You don't need a biology degree to understand this

01

Getting older quietly depletes your brain's fuel supply

From your mid-30s onwards, your body naturally produces less of the molecule your brain needs to generate energy. Most people don't notice it until it's been going on for years.

Think of your brain like a car engine. It needs fuel to run properly, and there's a molecule in your body (called NAD) that acts a bit like that fuel. The catch is that your body produces less and less of it every year from your mid-30s onwards.

By the time most people hit 50, they have roughly half the NAD levels they had in their 20s. The brain is one of the biggest consumers of this fuel, so when it starts running low, you feel it.

Your thinking feels slower than it used to
You forget words, names, or what you walked into a room for
Concentration takes more effort than it should
That "foggy" feeling that never quite clears
This isn't you "losing it" or getting old before your time. It's a fuel supply issue, and it can be supported.
02

Alcohol is harder on your brain energy than you'd think

Even moderate drinking, a couple of glasses in the evening, disrupts the very process your brain relies on to recover and restore its energy overnight.

Nobody's saying you have to stop drinking. But it's worth understanding what actually happens. When your body processes alcohol, it puts enormous strain on the same systems your brain uses to repair and recharge, essentially putting a big dent in your NAD levels while you sleep.

The next-day fogginess you write off as "just a bit tired" is often your brain struggling to generate enough energy after a night of playing catch-up.

Waking up unrefreshed even after a full night
That cloudy head in the morning that coffee barely touches
Mood that takes hours to lift
NAD Regen doesn't replace good habits, but it does help replenish what your body depletes, so the recovery is faster and the fog lifts sooner.
03

Poor sleep doesn't just leave you tired. It stops your brain cleaning itself.

Sleep is when your brain does its housekeeping: clearing out waste, restoring energy, and resetting for the next day. Without quality sleep, that process doesn't happen properly.

You probably know poor sleep makes you tired. What's less well known is that your brain uses the night to literally flush out waste products that build up during the day. This is when it resets and restores its energy stores.

When sleep is broken, too short, or shallow (whether from stress, kids, work, or just lying awake) that cleanup process is interrupted. You wake up and the slate hasn't been wiped clean.

Heavy-headed mornings regardless of hours slept
Relying on caffeine just to feel baseline normal
Mental fatigue that builds through the week
Supporting your NAD levels daily means giving your brain better raw material to work with, even on the mornings after the nights that weren't great.
04

Chronic stress quietly burns through your mental reserves

When your body is in stress mode, even low-level background stress, it pulls resources away from thinking clearly and towards just getting through. Over time, that depletes the fuel that keeps you sharp.

Modern stress is rarely short and sharp. It's long, grinding, and never fully off. Work pressures, family demands, financial worries, the endless news cycle. Your body treats all of it as an energy emergency, which means it burns through its reserves trying to keep you functional.

The brain is the biggest casualty. You find yourself:

Struggling to hold multiple things in your head at once
Making small mistakes you wouldn't usually make
Feeling mentally exhausted long before the day is done
Brain fog that's worst on your most stressful days
You can't always reduce the stress. But you can support the brain's ability to handle it, and that starts at the cellular level.
05

It compounds. Most people only notice once it's been going on for years.

Brain fog isn't usually dramatic. It's gradual. Each of these things layers on top of the other, and one day you realise you haven't felt truly sharp for a very long time.

The tricky thing about brain fog is how quietly it sneaks up on you. There's no single bad day where it starts. It's more like a dial that slowly turns down over a few years, until you realise you can't remember the last time your head felt completely clear.

By the time most people take action, it's already been building for years. The good news is that the body is remarkably responsive when you give it the right support, and it's never too late to start.

Thousands of people who've been living with brain fog for years have found that consistent daily support with NAD Regen makes a noticeable difference. Not overnight, but steadily and unmistakably. The most common thing people say? "I didn't realise how foggy I'd been until the fog started lifting."
